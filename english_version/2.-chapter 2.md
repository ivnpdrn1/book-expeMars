# **Chapter 2 — Identity and Access Control**

The system could sustain itself.

But it still did not know who could use it.

Valeria stated it without hesitation.

—Nothing activates without identity.

Malik reviewed the first layers.

Sensors. Deployed units. Stable perimeter.

Everything responded.

But it responded to any valid signal.

—That’s not control —he said.

—No —Valeria replied—. That’s exposure.

Silence.

On Earth, systems were protected by redundancy.

External layers. Distributed infrastructure. Protocols that assumed margin for error.

Mars offered no such margin.

Here, an incorrect activation was not an incident.

It was a consequence.

And consequences did not dissipate.

They accumulated.

—First layer —Valeria said—. Identity.

Malik began to define.

Not names.

Not traditional human roles.

Entities.

Operators. Autonomous units. Support modules.

Each had to exist within the system.

Not as a physical presence.

As a valid reference.

—If it cannot be identified —Malik said— it cannot operate.

Valeria nodded.

—And if it can operate without being identified…

—then the system stops being reliable.

Silence.

The first identity structure was activated.

Not visible.

But critical.

Every action now required an origin.

Every signal had to belong to something.

—This reduces speed —Malik said.

—It reduces error —Valeria replied.

Pause.

—Here, that matters more.

Silence.

The second layer followed immediately.

Authorization.

Not everything that exists can do everything.

Not everything that responds should decide.

—We define levels —Malik said.

—Not hierarchies —Valeria corrected—. Capabilities.

Silence.

The distinction mattered.

They were not replicating human structures.

They were assigning sustainable functions.

Some entities could activate.

Others could execute.

Others could only observe.

—Minimum principle —Valeria said.

Malik nodded.

—Each entity does only what is necessary.

—And nothing more.

Silence.

Because on Mars, excess is not an advantage.

It is risk.

A unit received a signal.

It attempted to execute an action outside its range.

The system blocked it.

—We didn’t explicitly program that —Malik said.

Valeria observed the response.

—No.

Pause.

—But it was necessary.

Silence.

That was what mattered.

The system was not just executing rules.

It was beginning to sustain coherence.

—If something can do more than it should… —Malik said.

—it eventually will —Valeria completed.

Silence.

And that “eventually” on Mars was not tolerable.

The third layer was integrated.

Location.

Not all decisions should be made everywhere.

Remote activation. Local control. Environmental dependency.

—Not everything should be centralized —Malik said.

—Nor everything distributed —Valeria replied.

Silence.

Balance was not optional.

It was structural.

A decision had to be executed close to the problem.

But not without validation.

And not without context.

—This is becoming more complex —Malik said.

—This is becoming more real —Valeria replied.

Silence.

The first tests began.

Valid identities. Limited access. Contained actions.

The system responded.

Not faster.

But more precise.

—We’re losing flexibility —Malik said.

—We’re gaining stability —Valeria corrected.

Silence.

That difference defined everything.

Flexibility allows adaptation.

But without limits… it generates inconsistency.

And inconsistency, in this environment, scales.

Malik observed the full flow.

—Now every action has an origin.

—And every origin has a limit —Valeria replied.

Pause.

—That’s what makes it sustainable.

Silence.

And then something small happened.

Almost imperceptible.

A sequence of decisions executed without conflict.

Not because there were no options.

Because none outside the defined range were considered.

—That’s not just access control —Malik said.

Valeria looked at him.

—No.

Pause.

—It’s a way of deciding.

Silence.

It didn’t have a name yet.

It didn’t need one.

But it was no longer just a set of rules.

It was a logic.

Something that was beginning to define not only who could act…

but how they had to do it.

Malik took a deep breath.

—This is going to scale.

Valeria nodded.

—It has to.

Silence.

Because if it didn’t…

everything they built on top of it

would have no way to sustain itself.

And in that moment…

without explicit recognition

without the need for validation

the system stopped being just an extension of those who operated it

and began to become

something that also

placed limits on them.
